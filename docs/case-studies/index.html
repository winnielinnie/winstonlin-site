OCI Platform Infrastructure

Deterministic Region Delivery

Turned regional delivery from reactive coordination into a deterministic orchestration system, helping compress timelines from months to about 30 days.

Problem

Regional buildouts depended on manual scheduling, service-specific runbooks, and late discovery of broken dependencies. The result was unpredictable timelines, repeated war rooms, and too much operational work happening in real time.

What I did

  • Helped frame the work as a product for deterministic orchestration rather than one more coordination layer on top of messy existing processes.
  • Pushed toward formal build specifications that made build-time dependencies, execution order, and rollback paths explicit enough to validate before execution.
  • Focused the design on testability, interventions, and high-confidence rollout sets so teams could catch breakage before it blocked a live regional build.

Why it was hard

  • The hard part was not automation alone. It was deciding what needed to become shared platform behavior versus what should stay a local service concern.
  • Region build systems accumulate exceptions quickly, so the product challenge was creating enough structure to make the plan deterministic without hard-coding every edge case.

Outcome

  • Regional delivery timelines dropped from about 3 months to roughly 30 days.
  • The work created a more predictable foundation for launch planning, earlier dependency validation, and far less real-time coordination during critical build windows.

OCI Platform Infrastructure

Service Dependency and Health Registry

Built an authoritative model for service dependencies, health, and ownership that helped cut root-cause analysis time by 60%.

Problem

When incidents or build failures happened, teams could usually see the symptom but not the true dependency path, ownership chain, or impact radius. Critical service data was fragmented, stale, and too dependent on tribal knowledge.

What I did

  • Helped shape the service around operational questions rather than turning it into a passive inventory or metadata dump.
  • Focused the model on versioned dependency contracts, health states, support attribution, and enough curation to keep the data trustworthy over time.
  • Worked backward from operator workflows so the product could answer the questions people actually have during incidents and blocked builds.

Why it was hard

  • A registry only matters if it stays current enough to be trusted under pressure.
  • The challenge was balancing a strong formal model with an onboarding path that service teams would actually adopt and maintain.

Outcome

  • Root-cause analysis time dropped by about 60%.
  • The work made ownership, dependency, and impact questions far more answerable during incidents, rollout debugging, and regional build operations.

OCI Functions

AI-Assisted OCI Functions Workflows

Worked on AI-assisted deployment and troubleshooting flows for OCI Functions to reduce setup drag and make platform recovery paths clearer.

Problem

A lot of friction in serverless adoption comes from the first-run experience: packaging, environment setup, troubleshooting, and the gap between a docs path and a working deployment.

What I did

  • Explored AI-assisted deployment and troubleshooting flows using MCP-style integrations and reusable skills.
  • Treated the problem as a workflow-design problem, not just a model-response problem.
  • Focused on making the output operationally useful: what should happen next, what failed, and how to recover.

Why it was hard

  • AI features are easy to demo and much harder to trust in real developer workflows.
  • The bar was not 'did the assistant say something plausible.' The bar was whether it reduced friction without creating new ambiguity.

Outcome

  • Created a credible path for AI-assisted setup and triage instead of treating AI as a sidecar novelty.
  • Sharpened my view that trustworthy AI products need strong recovery paths, explicit boundaries, and good workflow design.
